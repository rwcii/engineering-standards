# WDR 009: AI Provenance & Accountability

**Date:** 2026-05-31
**Status:** Accepted
**Authors:** Robert Capps

---

## Executive Summary

This repository forbids AI/bot attribution — the author of a change is the human.
That rule is right, but stated alone it assumes a human is always in the loop to
*be* the author, and it erases a fact that matters for incident response: that a
change was generated by an agent at all. Both gaps are resolved by separating two
ideas the topic usually conflates — **authorship/attribution** versus
**accountability**, and **human-in-the-loop** versus **autonomous** operation.

The principle: **accountability always resolves to a named human, in one of two
modes.** When a human is in the loop, that human reviews and *vouches* for the
change. When the agent runs **autonomously** — no human reviewing each change —
accountability rests with the **authorizing principal**: the human (or owning role)
on whose behalf the agent acts. Either way, the agent is never the accountable
party, the change records *which mode* produced it and *which human* stands behind
it, and a durable action trail keeps even unattended work traceable to that person.
Process provenance is recorded out-of-band, so the no-attribution rule stands while
the generation fact stays recoverable.

## 1. Problem Context

### The Problem

The cardinal rule "no AI/bot attribution; the author is the human" prevents a real
harm — laundering responsibility onto a tool, or polluting history with bot
co-author trailers. But it carries two unstated assumptions that break under
agentic use.

- **It assumes a human is always in the loop.** If "the author is the human" means
  a person read, understood, and vouched for every change, then *truly autonomous*
  operation — a scheduled, remote, or unattended agent acting on its own — is either
  forbidden or forced to masquerade as human-authored. Neither is acceptable:
  autonomy is a legitimate, intended mode, but accountability cannot evaporate
  because the human stepped back from each change. It must shift to a different
  human relationship, not disappear.
- **It is silent on provenance.** When most merged code is AI-assisted and nothing
  records which, incident response and regression hunts lose their single most
  useful fact — *was this generated, by what, and on whose authority?*

A third gap is orthogonal to both: for an agent the diff is the visible five
percent; the commands it ran, tools it called, and files it touched — the part that
matters most when a generated or autonomous change causes harm — leave no trace
unless one is kept.

The way through is to stop conflating distinct facts: *who is responsible* (always
a human) is separate from *how the change was produced* (which mode, AI-assisted or
not); and *human-in-the-loop* (a human vouches) is separate from *autonomous* (a
human delegated). Naming the modes is what leaves room for autonomy without giving
up an owner.

### Requirements

1. Responsibility for every change and consequential action traces to a named human
   — in both modes.
2. The production mode (human-in-the-loop vs. autonomous) is explicit and honestly
   recorded; the model must accommodate truly autonomous operation.
3. In autonomous mode, the authorizing principal and the bounds they authorized are
   on record — autonomy is never anonymous.
4. Consequential actions are reconstructable after the fact, and the main line stays
   revertible.

### Scope

Covers the accountability model across both operating modes, provenance, and the
agent action trail. The independent review that applies in *both* modes — and which
may itself be performed by an agent — is WDR 005; the autonomy tiers an autonomous
agent may exercise, and the escalation that halts it, are WDR 006; the
secrets-must-not-leak constraint bounding the trail's contents is WDR 007.

## 2. Decision 1: Accountability Always Resolves to a Named Human, in One of Two Modes

### Decision

Every change and every consequential action is accountable to a **named human** —
never to the agent. Operation runs in one of two explicitly recorded modes:

- **Human-in-the-loop** — a human **reviews and vouches** for the change before it
  lands; that human owns its correctness.
- **Autonomous (human-not-in-the-loop)** — no human reviews each change in real
  time; accountability rests with the **authorizing principal**: the human, or owning
  human role, who delegated the agent to act on their behalf and owns its outputs by
  virtue of that delegation.

The mode is recorded per change or run. The cardinal rule holds in both: authorship
is never assigned to a tool or agent. Independent review (WDR 005) is **orthogonal to
mode** and applies to both — autonomous is not a synonym for unreviewed; it means no
*human* voucher, with an independent (often agent-performed) review still in force.

### Alternatives Considered

| Alternative | Description | Why Rejected |
| ----------- | ----------- | ------------ |
| Attribute to the AI | Credit the agent as author/co-author | Launders responsibility onto something that cannot be held accountable; pollutes history with bot trailers |
| Require a human voucher for every change | Treat per-change human review as mandatory in all cases | Forecloses legitimate autonomous, unattended operation — a valuable and intended mode that does not have a human reading each change |
| Autonomous means unaccountable | Accept that unattended runs simply have no owner | Accountability cannot vanish because a human stepped back; an *unowned* change is the thing to forbid, not an unattended one |

### Rationale

1. Anchoring accountability to a human, never the tool, is what keeps responsibility
   real regardless of mode.
2. Naming two modes leaves explicit room for true autonomy while preserving a
   responsible owner for everything that ships.
3. Recording *which* mode produced a change makes the basis of accountability —
   vouching versus delegation — a stated fact rather than a silent assumption.

### Enforcement

Per project: the operating mode recorded per change/run; in human-in-the-loop mode
the human author owns the change and independent review (WDR 005) gates merge; no
bot attribution in authors, commits, or trailers under either mode.

## 3. Decision 2: Autonomous Operation Records Its Authorizing Principal and Mandate

### Decision

When the agent runs with **no human in the loop**, the change and the run record:

- the **authorizing principal** — the named human, or owning human role, on whose
  behalf the agent acted; and
- the **mandate** — the authorizing task, policy, or schedule that delegated the
  work, including the bounds it was permitted to act within (notably which autonomy
  tiers it may exercise without real-time confirmation, per WDR 006).

So that, even with no reviewer, "who is accountable for this?" and "what was it
authorized to do?" both have concrete, recorded answers.

### Alternatives Considered

| Alternative | Description | Why Rejected |
| ----------- | ----------- | ------------ |
| Record no principal | Let unattended runs proceed without a recorded owner | Accountability becomes anonymous exactly where it matters most — no human to trace an autonomous action back to |
| Principal is the service/bot account | Record the agent's machine identity as the actor | A service account cannot be accountable; the record must resolve to a human or an owning human role |
| Implicit principal | Assume "whoever configured it" without recording it | Unrecoverable after the fact; the link from an autonomous action to its responsible human must be explicit and durable |

### Rationale

1. A recorded principal is precisely what makes autonomous operation *accountable*
   rather than anonymous — the answer to "who stands behind this?"
2. Recording the mandate legitimizes and bounds what the agent did: it acted within
   a delegation, on the principal's authority, not on its own.
3. The mandate is where the principal pre-authorizes which action tiers (WDR 006) the
   agent may take unattended — converting WDR 006's "explicit human confirmation"
   into a recorded prior authorization for the autonomous case.

### Enforcement

Per project: autonomous runs carry the authorizing principal (human or owning role)
and the mandate/policy in their provenance and action trail; the mandate states the
permitted autonomy tiers; an autonomous action outside the mandate halts and
escalates (WDR 006).

## 4. Decision 3: Process Provenance Is Recorded Out-of-Band — Mode, Principal, AI-Assistance

### Decision

Process provenance — the production **mode** (human-in-the-loop / autonomous), the
**accountable human or principal**, and the fact the change was **AI-assisted** — is
recorded in machine-readable form, **out-of-band** from authorship (for example:
change metadata, a pull-request field, a generation manifest, or a notes ref), never
as a commit author or co-author trailer. Provenance answers "how, and on whose
authority, was this produced"; authorship answers "who is responsible" (always the
human). Recording the former does not violate the rule against the latter.

### Alternatives Considered

| Alternative | Description | Why Rejected |
| ----------- | ----------- | ------------ |
| Record nothing | Stay silent on provenance | Erases the safety-relevant facts — generated? autonomous? whose mandate? — for incident response and regression hunts |
| Provenance as bot co-authorship | Encode "AI-assisted" via a co-author trailer | Collides with the no-attribution rule and re-conflates provenance with authorship — the exact confusion this record removes |

### Rationale

1. Mode, principal, and AI-assistance are facts *about how the change was produced*,
   distinct from authorship; recording them out-of-band keeps the cardinal rule
   intact.
2. For an autonomous change, the mode and principal are the *most* safety-relevant
   facts to be able to recover.
3. Machine-readable provenance serves incident response, audits, and
   model-regression hunts without touching who is named as author.

### Enforcement

Per project: an out-of-band provenance channel (metadata / PR field / manifest /
notes ref) recording mode, accountable human/principal, and AI-assistance; never a
bot author or co-author trailer.

## 5. Decision 4: Consequential Agent Actions Leave a Durable Audit Trail

### Decision

Consequential agent actions are captured in a **durable, append-only trail**,
distinct from the code diff: the **authorizing principal and mandate** (for
autonomous runs), the task/prompt, tools invoked, commands run, files touched, and
the outcome. The trail **excludes secrets and full transcripts** (per WDR 007). Agent
work happens on **isolated branches/worktrees** so the main line is never directly
mutated and a clean revert is always available.

### Alternatives Considered

| Alternative | Description | Why Rejected |
| ----------- | ----------- | ------------ |
| The diff is the record | Rely on the committed change as the only history | The diff is the visible 5%; the commands and tool calls that caused harm leave no trace — and no link to the principal |
| Full transcript as the log | Keep the entire agent conversation as the trail | Bloated, and it captures secrets and noise; an append-only action summary is the forensic artifact, not the chat |
| Work directly on the main line | Let the agent commit straight to main | No clean revert; a bad generated or autonomous change is entangled with everything after it |

### Rationale

1. The action trail is the only forensic artifact when a generated or autonomous
   change causes harm — and, for autonomous runs, the only post-hoc link from an
   unattended action back to the human accountable for it.
2. An append-only summary (not the transcript) keeps the trail useful and safe to
   retain.
3. Branch/worktree isolation keeps revert a viable safety net, pairing with the
   scope-containment and autonomy guardrails (WDR 003, WDR 006).

### Enforcement

Per project: an append-only action log (authorizing principal/mandate, task, tools,
commands, files, outcome) with retention and explicit exclusions (no secrets, no
full transcript); agent work isolated on branches/worktrees off the main line.

---

## 6. Conclusion

Provenance and accountability are reconciled by separating the facts the topic
usually conflates. Accountability always resolves to a named human, but in one of two
modes: human-in-the-loop, where a human vouches for the change, and autonomous, where
the authorizing principal — the human the agent acts on behalf of — owns its outputs.
Truly autonomous operation is thereby first-class but never anonymous: the mode, the
principal, and the mandate are on record, an independent review (WDR 005) still
applies, and a durable, secret-free action trail links every unattended action back
to its responsible human. Authorship stays human and bot-attribution-free; process
provenance lives out-of-band so the generation fact remains recoverable. The concrete
provenance channel, principal/mandate representation, log format, and isolation
mechanics are each adopting project's enforcement.
